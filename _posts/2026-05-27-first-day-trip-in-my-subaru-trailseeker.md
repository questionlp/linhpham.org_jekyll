In my previous blog post, [Omnibus Update: April 2026 Edition]({{ site_url }}{% post_url 2026-04-30-omnibus-update-april-2026-edition %}), I mentioned that I would collect my thoughts on my [2026 Subaru Trailseeker](https://www.subaru.com/vehicles/trailseeker) after 3000 miles (4830 km). I am planning to do that write-up after having the car for 3 months rather than 3000 miles.

Having said that, I took the first day trip from Portland, Oregon to the [Mima Mounds Natural Area Preserve](https://dnr.wa.gov/natural-areas/natural-area-preserves/mima-mounds-natural-area-preserve) near Littlerock, Washington in the Trailseeker on Tuesday and wanted to document the DC fast charging experience there and back.

If you would like to visit Mima Mounds, note that a Washington State [Discover Pass](https://discoverpass.wa.gov/) is required. A single day pass costs 11.50 USD (fees included) and, if you frequently visit the state of Washington, or live in the state, an annual pass costs 50 USD (fees included).

### Preparing for the Trip

In the weeks leading up to and several days after getting the keys to my Trailseeker, I had already created accounts and downloaded apps for the various EV charging networks that are located in Oregon and Washington. Those charging networks include [ChargePoint](https://www.chargepoint.com/), [Electrify America](https://www.electrifyamerica.com/), and [EVgo](https://evgo.com/). I did the same for both [A Better Route Planner](https://abetterrouteplanner.com/) (ABRP) and [PlugShare](https://www.plugshare.com/).

Knowing that I would be using Electrify America DC fast charging stations regularly, I opted to get their Pass+ subscription for 7.00 USD per month to save 20% on charging rates. Even before this trip, I have already saved more than the 7.00 USD that I spent on the subscription.

As I was getting set up with ABRP, I noticed that they still do not have the 2026 Subaru Trailseeker, or its twin, the 2026 Toyota bZ Woodland, in their list of models that they have information for. Since I knew that my Trailseeker (Touring trim with 20-inch (50.8 cm) wheels) would have a little bit less range than a comparable 2026 Subaru Solterra with a similar trim and wheel trim, I selected the 2026 Subaru Solterra in the app and set the battery degradation value to 5% in hopes to make up the range difference.

Setting the value to 5% probably was a bit too high, as I would find out on the drive up.

The planned route that ABRP generated based on the parameters that the trip started with the Trailseeker starting the trip with 80% state of charge (SoC) and wanted to arrive at Mima Mounds with 55% SoC. The plan included a suggested 6 minute stop at a DC fast charging station around Ground Mound, Washington, about 25 miles (40.2 km) away from Mima Mounds.

The route from home, grabbing coffee and food to eat when getting to Mima Mounds, one charging stop, and arriving at Mima Mounds was around 135 miles (217 km).

### First Charging Stop

By the time I had crossed over the Columbia River, the SoC reading from the car was about 4% higher than the estimated SoC that ABRP displayed. As I got closer to Centralia, that gap had increased to 6%. Once I got to the first charging stop, the SoC gap between the car and ABRP had grown to 8%.

At the DC fast charging station, the chargers in the back of the station were placed ahead of each parking spot (in front of the car if parked nose in). The ones on the side of the lot were situated parallel with and in between each parking spot.

All of the 2026 Subaru and Toyota EVs currently sold in the United States have the charging port located on the front passenger fender. I wasn't sure that the charging cables in the back of the lot would reach when I parked nose in. So, I decided to pull into one of the spots on the side of the lot. Between the car, the bollards and the charger, there was just enough room to squeeze through to get to the cable and plug it into the car. It may be a bit more difficult for wider vehicles to squeeze into those spots and those may have to use the chargers in the back.

I initiated the charge through the app and decided to charge the Trailseeker for 12 minutes rather than the 6 minutes as suggested by ABRP. This brought the car up to 66% SoC with around 14 kWh dispensed (10.5 kWh delivered to the batteries). I chose to charge a little bit longer to provide a little buffer on the way back in case I wanted to venture around the area before heading back towards home.

### Seeing the Mima Mounds in Person

After wrapping up the charging session, I finished the drive up the Mima Mounds Natural Area Preserve. The access road from the main roads to the parking lot barely fits two mid-sized crossovers side-by-side when both passenger side wheels are off of the pavement. This was a touch hairy on the way out when a USGS (United States Geological Survey) vehicle was heading in while I was heading out after the visit.

Going out to the mounds and walking around the designated gravel or paved paths was quite an experience. When I was walking around, there wasn't anyone else in the area. All you could hear were the footsteps on the gravel, birds chirping, bees buzzing around and a slight breeze. Oh, and some sounds of the shooting range located on the other side of the preserve. It wasn't until we rounded back to the parking lot did we see two folks heading in.

I've uploaded some of the photos to Flickr under the [26 May 2026 Mima Mounds Natural Area Preserve](https://www.flickr.com/photos/questionlp/albums/72177720333894269) album.

### Heading Back and Second Charging Stop

On the way back, I decided to try out an EVgo charging station since I had 5.00 USD credit with them when I signed up for an account when I got my Trailseeker. When I got to the parking lot in Chehalis, Washington with the EVgo chargers, I noticed that one side of the lot had a row of older generation Tesla Superchargers all placed ahead of each parking spot. Just to see if the charging cables could actually reach when pulled in normally, I pulled as far foward as I was comfortable with, came out, undocked the connector and tried to plug it into the car. As expected, it would not reach by a good 3 feet (approximately 1 meter). Good that I could validate my assumptions and what I have heard and seen from other folks online.

So, I backed out of the spot and pulled into an open EVgo charging spot, in between a [Chevrolet BrightDrop electric van](https://www.chevrolet.com/commercial/brightdrop) and a Hyundai Ioniq 6. The charger had one cable for CCS1 and one cable for CHAdeMO and supported both to be used at the same time. As I was getting the CCS1 to NACS adapter that came with my car, I tried finding the charging station using the EVgo app. No matter how many times I zoomed into the location (Chehalis Commerce Center) and restarted the app, the charging stations would not load up or come up on the in-app search.

I don't know if it was my phone's cell network connection that was acting up or the app had something cached. It wasn't until I got home and restarted the EVgo app one more time did the station finally come up when searching for it.

So, I decided to use Apple Pay to pay for the charging session to get from 55% to 70% SoC with 9.86 kWh delivered to the batteries in 11.5 minutes. Unfortunately, I could not read the display very well (dim screen along with the protective plastic cover) to see how much was dispensed or the total cost. Not exactly a great first time experience with EVgo. I requested an EVgo NFC card so that I am not dependent on my phone or the app working in order to start a charging session.

### Third Charging Stop and an Obligatory Visit to IKEA

After unplugging from the EVgo charger, I made the 86 mile (138.4 km) drive from Chehalis down to the Portland IKEA at Cascade Station. I chose to stop at IKEA so that I could charge the car at the Electrify America chargers and to go into IKEA to get some stuff from the food market (no new [BLÅHAJ](https://www.ikea.com/us/en/p/blahaj-soft-toy-shark-90373590/) for me this time). The Trailseeker was at 36% SoC when I plugged it in and went to do my shopping.

Once I got back out the car, the charger had dispensed 35.92 kWh in 26.3 minutes (with a 96 kW max charging speed) that it was charging for. The cost, with the Electrify America Pass+ discount, came to 15.07 USD. Of the 35.92 kWh dispensed, the batteries took in 31.54 kWh and brought them up to 84% SoC.

Afer that, I made the trek back home all safe and sound with 77% SoC. The route back from Mima Mounds to home, including a stop at IKEA, and some small detours around bad spots of traffic in Portland, clocked in at around 145 miles (233.4 km).

### Closing Thoughts

Outside of the hiccup with the EVgo app for the second charging session, the overall experience of taking an EV on a day trip wasn't too different from when I took day trips in my previous Audis. Of the two charging sessions where I stayed in the car, they were both around 12 minutes. During that time, I went out to stretch, walk around a little bit, snacked, and felt refreshed for the next leg of the trip.

I'm really glad that both Electify America and EVgo chargers that I used support paying for charging sessions using credit cards and using the app (directly or using a digital wallet). I'm not a huge fan of charging networks that only support starting sessions using an app. Although it may be more convenient to use Plug & Charge to make the charging experience much quicker, but I don't think I would be able to take advantage of any discounts that I have with Electrify America or any other charging networks that support it.

On both legs of the trip, the average efficiency reported by the Trailseeker was around 3.1 miles/kWh (5 km/kWh or 20 kWh/100 km), which included long stretches of highway and freeway driving. I made use of the adaptive cruise control along with setting the regenerative braking setting to as high as possible. That closely matches the [EPA's 103 MPGe highway rating](https://fueleconomy.gov/feg/Find.do?action=sbs&id=50299) (4.92 km/kWh or 20.33 kWh/100 km) for the Trailseeker and nearly matches the weighted average of 80% highway and 20% city driving.

The adaptive cruise control with assisted steering helped reduce the tired feeling that comes with driving longer stretches of road. I still wished Subaru would allow people to select the standard 18-inch (45.7 cm) wheels instead of the 20-inch (50.8 cm) wheels when choosing the Touring trim. The larger wheels require lower-profile tires that makes the ride harsher on bumpy and rough road surfaces.

As I am writing this, I haven't made any plans for the next day trip, though a visit to the Oregon coast is up for consideration.
